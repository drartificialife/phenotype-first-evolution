COVERING LETTER FOR AMERICAN NATURALIST SUBMISSION

Manuscript Title: "Organisms Write Their Own Evolution: Direct Epigenomic Inheritance Accelerates Learning on Complex Tasks"

════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

WHY THIS MANUSCRIPT IS A GOOD FIT FOR AMERICAN NATURALIST:

This manuscript directly addresses a fundamental question at the heart of modern evolutionary biology: the extent to which organisms actively shape their own evolution. The work bridges computational modeling with evolutionary theory, testing Denis Noble's hypothesis about "organism agency" — the idea that living systems can directly read AND write to their heredity. This represents a significant challenge to purely gene-centric views of inheritance and is central to debates within the Extended Evolutionary Synthesis (EES).

American Naturalist has long been the premier venue for work examining the mechanisms and limits of evolutionary processes. This manuscript contributes to that tradition by:

1. THEORETICAL SIGNIFICANCE: Testing a novel hypothesis (direct epigenomic inheritance) against classical alternatives (selection-only, Baldwin Effect). The results provide quantitative evidence for when organism agency matters and when it doesn't—revealing both the power and limits of extended agency.

2. COMPUTATIONAL RIGOR: 540 experimental runs (30 replicates × 6 tasks × 3 algorithms) with statistical significance testing (Welch's t-tests, p < 0.001). The study is reproducible, with complete code and data available on GitHub.

3. BIOLOGICAL RELEVANCE: The model incorporates epigenomic inheritance, which is documented in real biological systems (DNA methylation, histone modifications). The distinction from Lamarckism is critical: we model regulatory layer (epigenome) inheritance, not genetic inheritance, which is biologically plausible.

4. CLEAR MECHANISTIC INSIGHTS: The findings reveal that extended agency provides dramatic advantage (3-6×) on compatible learning problems (L+T, T+Plus) but no advantage on geometrically incompatible problems (L+Plus). This demonstrates that organism agency is powerful but constrained—a nuanced finding that advances theoretical understanding.

5. COMPOSITION PROBLEM FRAMEWORK: The work connects to ongoing discussions in American Naturalist about how complex traits evolve—showing that inheritance of learned components enables compositional learning in ways selection alone cannot achieve.
